Why Comic Sans is Actually Genius (Sometimes)

Aa

Jack Spider

March 15, 2024

It is the villain of the design world. The font that launched a thousand memes. Use it on a resume, and you're instantly disqualified. Use it on a storefront, and designers weep. But is Comic Sans really that bad? Or have we just been bullied into hating it?

Today, we are taking a deep dive into the history of Vincent Connare's creation, and why, in specific contexts (like accessibility and actual comic books), it might just be the hero we didn't know we needed.

The Origin Story

In 1994, Microsoft was developing an interface called Microsoft Bob. The character, a dog named Rover, spoke in speech bubbles. However, the text inside was Times New Roman. Connare saw this mismatch—a cartoon dog speaking like a 19th-century newspaper—and decided to fix it.

"If you love it, you don't know much about typography. If you hate it, you really don't know much about typography." — Vincent Connare

Why Designers Hate It

  • It breaks the grid system (irregular kerning).
  • It was overused in serious contexts (gravestones, warnings, legal docs).
  • It represents "amateur" design.

The Redemption Arc

Despite the hate, Comic Sans has a superpower: Readability. Because of its distinct letter shapes (look at the 'b' and 'd', they aren't just mirror images), it is incredibly friendly for dyslexic readers.

CSS Example
.font-comic {
  font-family: 'Comic Sans MS', 'Chalkboard SE', sans-serif;
  letter-spacing: 0.05em;
  /* The forbidden fruit of typography */
}

So, the next time you see Comic Sans on a lemonade stand sign, don't sneer. Salute it. It's doing exactly what it was designed to do: be friendly, informal, and legible.
